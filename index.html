Banned Books in America

A Chronicle of Literary Censorship

About Book Banning in the United States

Book banning and censorship in the United States has a long and troubling history. A book challenge is an attempt to remove or restrict materials based on the objections of a person or group. A book ban is the actual removal of those materials. Challenges do not simply involve a person expressing a point of view; rather, they are an attempt to remove material from the curriculum or library, thereby restricting the access of others.

"Censorship is telling a man he can't have a steak just because a baby can't chew it." — Mark Twain

According to the American Library Association (ALA), there has been a dramatic increase in book challenges and bans in recent years. In 2022 alone, over 1,200 unique titles were challenged, the highest number of attempted book bans since the ALA began compiling data about censorship in libraries more than 20 years ago. These challenges predominantly target books by or about people of color and LGBTQ+ individuals.

Books are typically challenged for containing materials deemed "sexually explicit," having "offensive language," being "unsuited to age group," containing "LGBTQ+ content," featuring "religious viewpoint," discussing "violence," addressing "racism," or exploring "political viewpoint." Often, books that address difficult topics like racism, sexual abuse, or LGBTQ+ experiences are targeted precisely because they give voice to marginalized communities and validate the experiences of young readers who see themselves reflected in these stories.

The First Amendment protects freedom of speech and expression, which includes the right to read. While parents certainly have the right to guide their own children's reading, book bans in schools and libraries restrict access for all students and community members. They represent a form of censorship that limits intellectual freedom and the free exchange of ideas.

The Impact of Book Banning

Book banning has profound effects on education, literacy, and democratic values. When books are removed from libraries and classrooms, students lose access to diverse perspectives, historical contexts, and stories that might help them understand themselves and others. Censorship particularly impacts marginalized communities whose stories are most often targeted for removal.

Librarians and educators face increasing pressure and threats when defending intellectual freedom. Many report self-censorship, avoiding purchasing books that might be challenged, which further limits access to diverse literature. Teachers modify curricula to avoid controversy, and some have faced termination for refusing to remove books from their classrooms.

Banned Classic Literature

Even works now considered essential to American literature have faced censorship throughout history. These books challenged prevailing norms, addressed controversial topics, or used language deemed inappropriate for their time.

To Kill a Mockingbird
by Harper Lee (1960)
Challenged for: Racial slurs, discussion of rape, offensive language

This Pulitzer Prize-winning novel exploring racism in the American South has been one of the most challenged books despite its anti-racist message. Critics object to the historical use of racial slurs in the text, while defenders argue the book provides crucial context for discussing America's racist past and present.

The Catcher in the Rye
by J.D. Salinger (1951)
Challenged for: Profanity, sexual references, blasphemy, undermining of values

This iconic coming-of-age novel has been banned and challenged since its publication. Its frank depiction of teenage angst, alienation, and rebellion has made it both beloved by readers and targeted by censors who view it as corrupting to youth.

1984
by George Orwell (1949)
Challenged for: Political themes, sexual content, pro-communist ideology

Ironically, this dystopian novel about totalitarian censorship and thought control has itself been censored. It has been challenged by both conservative groups for sexual content and leftist governments for its critique of authoritarianism.

The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn
by Mark Twain (1884)
Challenged for: Racial slurs, coarse language, depiction of race relations

Mark Twain's masterpiece satirizing racism in pre-Civil War America has been continuously challenged since publication. While Twain intended the novel as an indictment of slavery and racism, its use of period-accurate racist language has led to numerous challenges and bans.

Of Mice and Men
by John Steinbeck (1937)
Challenged for: Profanity, racial slurs, violence

Steinbeck's novella about migrant workers during the Great Depression remains one of the most challenged books. Its realistic dialogue, including profanity and racial slurs, has led to repeated attempts to remove it from school curricula.

Brave New World
by Aldous Huxley (1932)
Challenged for: Sexual content, drug use, insensitivity to Indigenous peoples

This dystopian novel exploring themes of technology, conditioning, and free will has been challenged for its depictions of casual sex, drug use, and suicide. Critics have also objected to its use of "savage" to describe Indigenous peoples.

The Great Gatsby
by F. Scott Fitzgerald (1925)
Challenged for: Sexual references, language, portrayal of adultery

This quintessential American novel has faced challenges for its references to adultery, its language, and what some consider an irreverent attitude toward marriage and traditional values.

The Color Purple
by Alice Walker (1982)
Challenged for: Sexual content, violence, offensive language, homosexuality

This Pulitzer Prize-winning novel about African American women in the early 20th century South has been repeatedly challenged for its frank depictions of sexual abuse, violence, and lesbian relationships, despite its powerful message about resilience and self-discovery.

Slaughterhouse-Five
by Kurt Vonnegut (1969)
Challenged for: Profanity, violence, obscenity, anti-war sentiment

Vonnegut's anti-war novel combining science fiction with the author's experiences as a prisoner of war has been challenged for its profanity, sexual references, and perceived anti-American or anti-Christian sentiment.

"The books that the world calls immoral are books that show the world its own shame." — Oscar Wilde

Banned Contemporary Adult Fiction

Contemporary novels continue to face censorship challenges, often for addressing current social issues, depicting diverse experiences, or including content deemed sexually explicit or politically controversial.

The Handmaid's Tale
by Margaret Atwood (1985)
Challenged for: Sexual content, anti-Christian themes, profanity

This dystopian novel about a totalitarian theocracy that subjugates women has been challenged for its frank depictions of sexual violence and what some view as anti-religious content, though Atwood has stated the novel is a warning about extremism, not a critique of faith itself.

Beloved
by Toni Morrison (1987)
Challenged for: Violence, sexual content, depiction of bestiality

Morrison's Pulitzer Prize-winning novel about the psychological trauma of slavery has been challenged for its intense depictions of violence and sexual abuse, despite being widely considered one of the most important American novels.

The Kite Runner
by Khaled Hosseini (2003)
Challenged for: Sexual violence, offensive language, religious viewpoint

This novel about friendship and redemption in Afghanistan has been challenged for its depiction of sexual assault, particularly of a child, and for its portrayal of Afghan culture and Islam.

Thirteen Reasons Why
by Jay Asher (2007)
Challenged for: Discussion of suicide, sexual assault, drug use

This novel about a teen's suicide and the events leading to it has been challenged for potentially encouraging suicide, despite many mental health professionals and educators viewing it as an opportunity to discuss teen mental health.

Speak
by Laurie Halse Anderson (1999)
Challenged for: Sexual content, profanity, discussion of rape

This acclaimed novel about a high school student dealing with the aftermath of sexual assault has been repeatedly challenged, despite many survivors crediting the book with helping them process their own experiences and find their voice.

"Books are the most patient and faithful of all friends; they have the knack of remaining quiet while the reader is given time to think." — Mortimer J. Adler

Banned Young Adult Literature

Young adult literature faces particularly intense scrutiny and censorship efforts, with challengers often citing concerns about age-appropriateness. However, many of these books address issues that young people themselves face daily.

The Perks of Being a Wallflower
by Stephen Chbosky (1999)
Challenged for: Homosexuality, drug use, sexual content, profanity

This coming-of-age epistolary novel has been challenged for its frank discussions of mental health, sexuality, and teen experiences with drugs and alcohol. Supporters argue these honest depictions help teens feel less alone.

The Absolutely True Diary of a Part-Time Indian
by Sherman Alexie (2007)
Challenged for: Profanity, sexual references, offensive language, anti-Christian content

This semi-autobiographical novel about a Native American teenager has been frequently challenged despite its National Book Award and its authentic portrayal of contemporary Native American life.

Looking for Alaska
by John Green (2005)
Challenged for: Sexual content, smoking, drinking, profanity

Green's debut novel about teenagers at a boarding school has been challenged for depicting teen drinking, smoking, and sexual activity, despite addressing grief, friendship, and the search for meaning.

The Hate U Give
by Angie Thomas (2017)
Challenged for: Drug use, profanity, discussion of police brutality

This powerful novel about a Black teenager who witnesses a police shooting has been challenged for its language and portrayal of law enforcement, despite critical acclaim for its honest depiction of systemic racism and police violence.

Drama
by Raina Telgemeier (2012)
Challenged for: LGBTQ+ characters, perceived sexual content

This graphic novel about a middle school theater production has been one of the most challenged books simply for including LGBTQ+ characters, despite the content being appropriate for its intended middle-grade audience.

The Bluest Eye
by Toni Morrison (1970)
Challenged for: Sexual content, child sexual abuse, offensive language

Morrison's first novel about a young Black girl's desire for blue eyes has been challenged for its unflinching depiction of child sexual abuse and internalized racism, though advocates argue these difficult topics deserve serious literary treatment.

"A book is a loaded gun in the house next door. Burn it. Take the shot from the weapon." — Ray Bradbury, Fahrenheit 451

Banned Children's Books

Even books written for the youngest readers face censorship. Picture books and early readers have been challenged for diverse representation, acknowledgment of difficult topics, or perceived political messaging.

And Tango Makes Three
by Justin Richardson and Peter Parnell (2005)
Challenged for: Homosexuality, same-sex parenting

Based on a true story of two male penguins in New York's Central Park Zoo who raised a chick together, this picture book has been one of the most challenged books for over a decade. Critics object to its positive portrayal of same-sex parenting.

Captain Underpants series
by Dav Pilkey (1997-present)
Challenged for: Offensive language, unsuited for age group, violence

This hugely popular series has been challenged for potty humor, perceived disrespect for authority, and later books' inclusion of a gay character. Despite challenges, the books have been credited with encouraging reluctant readers.

Where the Wild Things Are
by Maurice Sendak (1963)
Challenged for: Encouraging children's disobedience, dark themes

This classic picture book faced challenges when first published for depicting a child being disobedient and sent to bed without supper. Critics worried it would encourage bad behavior, while supporters saw it as validating children's emotions.

The Giving Tree
by Shel Silverstein (1964)
Challenged for: Sexism, portrayal of unhealthy relationships

Silverstein's tale of a tree's devotion to a boy has been challenged for depicting what some see as an unhealthy, one-sided relationship that could teach children to accept exploitation.

A Light in the Attic
by Shel Silverstein (1981)
Challenged for: Encourages disobedience, occult references

This poetry collection has been challenged for poems that allegedly encourage children to break dishes, be messy, or talk back to parents. Some challengers have even claimed certain poems promote Satan worship.

Harry Potter series
by J.K. Rowling (1997-2007)
Challenged for: Witchcraft, occult themes, anti-family messages

Despite being one of the most beloved children's book series ever written, Harry Potter has been consistently challenged for its depictions of wizardry and magic, which some religious groups view as promoting occultism.

In the Night Kitchen
by Maurice Sendak (1970)
Challenged for: Nudity (child depicted naked)

This surreal picture book has been challenged and even had pages physically altered in some libraries because it depicts a young boy naked. Sendak defended the innocence of childhood nudity in the context of a dream sequence.

"You don't have to burn books to destroy a culture. Just get people to stop reading them." — Ray Bradbury

Books with LGBTQ+ Themes

Books featuring LGBTQ+ characters or themes face disproportionate censorship. According to PEN America, 41% of books banned in the 2021-2022 school year contained LGBTQ+ characters or themes. These challenges deny LGBTQ+ youth the opportunity to see themselves reflected in literature and prevent all readers from accessing diverse perspectives.

Gender Queer: A Memoir
by Maia Kobabe (2019)
Challenged for: LGBTQ+ content, sexual content, not suited for age group

This graphic memoir about the author's journey with gender identity has been the most challenged book in America for multiple years. It has been targeted in coordinated campaigns despite being placed in high school libraries and addressing common questions about gender.

All Boys Aren't Blue
by George M. Johnson (2020)
Challenged for: LGBTQ+ content, sexual content, profanity

This memoir exploring the author's childhood and adolescence as a Black queer person has been targeted in numerous states. Johnson has spoken out about how banning their book silences Black queer voices and denies LGBTQ+ youth of color the representation they need.

The Perks of Being a Wallflower
by Stephen Chbosky (1999)
Challenged for: Homosexuality, sexual content, drug use

This coming-of-age novel featuring LGBTQ+ characters has been challenged for years, with particular focus on its positive portrayal of a gay character and frank discussions about sexuality and identity.

This Book Is Gay
by Juno Dawson (2014)
Challenged for: LGBTQ+ content, sexual content, political viewpoint

This non-fiction guide for LGBTQ+ teens has been challenged despite providing factual information about sexual orientation and gender identity. The book addresses common questions from teens exploring their identity.

I Am Jazz
by Jessica Herthel and Jazz Jennings (2014)
Challenged for: Transgender character, age-inappropriate sex education

This picture book based on the real life of transgender activist Jazz Jennings has been challenged in elementary schools despite its age-appropriate content simply explaining what it means to be transgender.

George (now published as Melissa)
by Alex Gino (2015)
Challenged for: Transgender character, sexual content (none present)

This middle-grade novel about a transgender girl has been one of the most challenged books despite containing no sexual content. It simply tells the story of a young person understanding and expressing their gender identity.

Fun Home: A Family Tragicomic
by Alison Bechdel (2006)
Challenged for: LGBTQ+ content, sexual content, unsuited for age group

This acclaimed graphic memoir about Bechdel's relationship with her gay father and her own coming out has been challenged in college classrooms, sparking debates about intellectual freedom in higher education.

"Once you learn to read, you will be forever free." — Frederick Douglass

Books Addressing Race and Identity

Books by authors of color and books addressing racism, particularly anti-Black racism, face disproportionate challenges. According to PEN America's analysis, 40% of banned book titles feature characters of color or address race and racism. These bans often occur under the guise of protecting children from "divisive concepts" or "critical race theory," but they effectively silence discussions about America's racial history and present.

The Bluest Eye
by Toni Morrison (1970)
Challenged for: Sexual content, offensive language, depictions of child abuse

Morrison's debut novel exploring internalized racism and colorism in the Black community has been challenged for its unflinching portrayal of sexual abuse, though advocates note the book's literary merit and importance in discussing racial trauma.

The Hate U Give
by Angie Thomas (2017)
Challenged for: Profanity, drug use, anti-police message

This novel about a Black teenager who witnesses police shoot her unarmed friend has been challenged for being "anti-police" and containing profanity, despite its nuanced treatment of community, family, and activism.

Between the World and Me
by Ta-Nehisi Coates (2015)
Challenged for: Anti-police message, political viewpoint, profanity

Written as a letter to his son, Coates's meditation on being Black in America has been removed from reading lists for allegedly being "anti-police" and promoting a particular political viewpoint.

Stamped: Racism, Antiracism, and You
by Jason Reynolds and Ibram X. Kendi (2020)
Challenged for: Political viewpoint, selective storytelling, author's public statements

This young adult adaptation of Kendi's "Stamped from the Beginning" traces the history of racist ideas in America. It has been challenged as part of broader attempts to ban discussions of systemic racism in schools.

New Kid
by Jerry Craft (2019)
Challenged for: Critical race theory, discussion of racism

This Newbery Medal-winning graphic novel about a Black student's experiences at a predominantly white private school has been challenged for addressing racism and microaggressions, despite being appropriate for its middle-grade audience.

Born a Crime: Stories from a South African Childhood
by Trevor Noah (2016)
Challenged for: Profanity, mature themes, racial discussions

Noah's memoir about growing up in apartheid and post-apartheid South Africa has been challenged for its frank discussions of race, colonialism, and poverty, as well as its language.

The 1619 Project: Born on the Water
by Nikole Hannah-Jones and Renée Watson (2021)
Challenged for: Critical race theory, historical inaccuracy, political viewpoint

This children's book explaining American slavery history has been caught up in broader political battles over how slavery and racism should be taught in schools.

American Born Chinese
by Gene Luen Yang (2006)
Challenged for: Racial stereotypes, offensive language, religious viewpoint

This groundbreaking graphic novel exploring the Asian American experience has been challenged for its use of stereotypical imagery, even though the book explicitly addresses and critiques these stereotypes.

"There is no greater agony than bearing an untold story inside you." — Maya Angelou

How to Fight Book Banning

Individuals can take action to support intellectual freedom and oppose censorship in their communities. Here are ways to get involved:

Support Your Local Library

  • Attend library board meetings and speak up for intellectual freedom
  • Support librarians facing pressure to remove books
  • Check out challenged books to show they're valued by the community
  • Donate to library foundations that support diverse collections
  • Volunteer to support library programs and services

Get Involved in Your School District

  • Attend school board meetings and speak during public comment periods
  • Learn your district's book challenge process and participate in review committees
  • Support teachers and school librarians facing pressure
  • Run for school board or support candidates who defend intellectual freedom
  • Connect with other parents who support diverse, inclusive education

Advocate and Educate

  • Write letters to the editor of local newspapers
  • Contact your state and local representatives
  • Share information about book banning on social media
  • Organize community conversations about intellectual freedom
  • Participate in Banned Books Week events

Understanding Your Rights

The First Amendment protects freedom of speech and expression, including the right to read. While parents have the right to guide their own children's reading, they do not have the right to restrict what other families can access. Courts have consistently held that school libraries serve an important educational function and that the removal of books must not be done to suppress ideas.

In the landmark case Board of Education, Island Trees Union Free School District No. 26 v. Pico (1982), the Supreme Court held that school officials cannot remove books from libraries "simply because they dislike the ideas contained in those books."

Stay Informed

Book banning efforts are constantly evolving. Stay informed about challenges in your community and state by:

  • Following local news coverage of school board meetings
  • Subscribing to newsletters from anti-censorship organizations
  • Joining community groups focused on education and civil liberties
  • Monitoring your state legislature for bills restricting teaching or library collections
