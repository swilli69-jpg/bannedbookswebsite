How to Fight Book Banning

Individuals can take action to support intellectual freedom and oppose censorship in their communities. Here are ways to get involved:

Support Your Local Library

  • Attend library board meetings and speak up for intellectual freedom
  • Support librarians facing pressure to remove books
  • Check out challenged books to show they're valued by the community
  • Donate to library foundations that support diverse collections
  • Volunteer to support library programs and services

Get Involved in Your School District

  • Attend school board meetings and speak during public comment periods
  • Learn your district's book challenge process and participate in review committees
  • Support teachers and school librarians facing pressure
  • Run for school board or support candidates who defend intellectual freedom
  • Connect with other parents who support diverse, inclusive education

Advocate and Educate

  • Write letters to the editor of local newspapers
  • Contact your state and local representatives
  • Share information about book banning on social media
  • Organize community conversations about intellectual freedom
  • Participate in Banned Books Week events

Understanding Your Rights

The First Amendment protects freedom of speech and expression, including the right to read. While parents have the right to guide their own children's reading, they do not have the right to restrict what other families can access. Courts have consistently held that school libraries serve an important educational function and that the removal of books must not be done to suppress ideas.

In the landmark case Board of Education, Island Trees Union Free School District No. 26 v. Pico (1982), the Supreme Court held that school officials cannot remove books from libraries "simply because they dislike the ideas contained in those books."

Stay Informed

Book banning efforts are constantly evolving. Stay informed about challenges in your community and state by:

  • Following local news coverage of school board meetings
  • Subscribing to newsletters from anti-censorship organizations
  • Joining community groups focused on education and civil liberties
  • Monitoring your state legislature for bills restricting teaching or library collections
