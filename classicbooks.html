Banned Classic Literature

Even works now considered essential to American literature have faced censorship throughout history. These books challenged prevailing norms, addressed controversial topics, or used language deemed inappropriate for their time.

To Kill a Mockingbird
by Harper Lee (1960)
Challenged for: Racial slurs, discussion of rape, offensive language

This Pulitzer Prize-winning novel exploring racism in the American South has been one of the most challenged books despite its anti-racist message. Critics object to the historical use of racial slurs in the text, while defenders argue the book provides crucial context for discussing America's racist past and present.

The Catcher in the Rye
by J.D. Salinger (1951)
Challenged for: Profanity, sexual references, blasphemy, undermining of values

This iconic coming-of-age novel has been banned and challenged since its publication. Its frank depiction of teenage angst, alienation, and rebellion has made it both beloved by readers and targeted by censors who view it as corrupting to youth.

1984
by George Orwell (1949)
Challenged for: Political themes, sexual content, pro-communist ideology

Ironically, this dystopian novel about totalitarian censorship and thought control has itself been censored. It has been challenged by both conservative groups for sexual content and leftist governments for its critique of authoritarianism.

The Adventures of Huckleberry Finn
by Mark Twain (1884)
Challenged for: Racial slurs, coarse language, depiction of race relations

Mark Twain's masterpiece satirizing racism in pre-Civil War America has been continuously challenged since publication. While Twain intended the novel as an indictment of slavery and racism, its use of period-accurate racist language has led to numerous challenges and bans.

Of Mice and Men
by John Steinbeck (1937)
Challenged for: Profanity, racial slurs, violence

Steinbeck's novella about migrant workers during the Great Depression remains one of the most challenged books. Its realistic dialogue, including profanity and racial slurs, has led to repeated attempts to remove it from school curricula.

Brave New World
by Aldous Huxley (1932)
Challenged for: Sexual content, drug use, insensitivity to Indigenous peoples

This dystopian novel exploring themes of technology, conditioning, and free will has been challenged for its depictions of casual sex, drug use, and suicide. Critics have also objected to its use of "savage" to describe Indigenous peoples.

The Great Gatsby
by F. Scott Fitzgerald (1925)
Challenged for: Sexual references, language, portrayal of adultery

This quintessential American novel has faced challenges for its references to adultery, its language, and what some consider an irreverent attitude toward marriage and traditional values.

The Color Purple
by Alice Walker (1982)
Challenged for: Sexual content, violence, offensive language, homosexuality

This Pulitzer Prize-winning novel about African American women in the early 20th century South has been repeatedly challenged for its frank depictions of sexual abuse, violence, and lesbian relationships, despite its powerful message about resilience and self-discovery.

Slaughterhouse-Five
by Kurt Vonnegut (1969)
Challenged for: Profanity, violence, obscenity, anti-war sentiment

Vonnegut's anti-war novel combining science fiction with the author's experiences as a prisoner of war has been challenged for its profanity, sexual references, and perceived anti-American or anti-Christian sentiment.
