Banned Books about Race and Identity

Books that address race, racism, and cultural identity have become some of the most frequently challenged titles in the United States. These works often explore histories of inequality, celebrate marginalized communities, or offer students perspectives not widely represented in traditional curricula. However, they are increasingly targeted by groups claiming such books are divisive, “political,” or inappropriate for young readers. As a result, race- and identity-based texts sit at the center of contemporary debates over how we teach history and whose stories are valued in public education.

Many of the books being challenged are critically acclaimed and widely taught. Titles like The Hate U Give by Angie Thomas, The Color Purple by Alice Walker, and Song of Solomon by Toni Morrison. Morrison and other authors are often removed because they confront systemic racism directly or highlight the lived experiences of Black, Indigenous, Latino, Asian American, and other marginalized communities. In some districts, even books that merely feature characters of color—without explicit discussions of race—have faced scrutiny simply for expanding representation.

The consequences of banning these books reach far beyond the classroom. When students are denied access to literature rooted in diverse racial and cultural experiences, they lose opportunities to develop empathy, understand historical context, and engage critically with the world. For students of color, these bans can be especially damaging, reinforcing feelings of invisibility by signaling that their histories and identities are unwelcome or controversial.

Despite the rise in challenges, many educators, librarians, students, and families continue to advocate for inclusive reading materials. Community coalitions, professional organizations, and civil rights groups are working to protect access to these books and to ensure that students encounter a full and honest portrayal of American history. The fight over race- and identity-based banned books underscores an essential truth: equitable education depends on diverse stories being told—and protected.
