Books Addressing Race and Identity

Books by authors of color and books addressing racism, particularly anti-Black racism, face disproportionate challenges. According to PEN America's analysis, 40% of banned book titles feature characters of color or address race and racism. These bans often occur under the guise of protecting children from "divisive concepts" or "critical race theory," but they effectively silence discussions about America's racial history and present.

The Bluest Eye
by Toni Morrison (1970)
Challenged for: Sexual content, offensive language, depictions of child abuse

Morrison's debut novel exploring internalized racism and colorism in the Black community has been challenged for its unflinching portrayal of sexual abuse, though advocates note the book's literary merit and importance in discussing racial trauma.

The Hate U Give
by Angie Thomas (2017)
Challenged for: Profanity, drug use, anti-police message

This novel about a Black teenager who witnesses police shoot her unarmed friend has been challenged for being "anti-police" and containing profanity, despite its nuanced treatment of community, family, and activism.

Between the World and Me
by Ta-Nehisi Coates (2015)
Challenged for: Anti-police message, political viewpoint, profanity

Written as a letter to his son, Coates's meditation on being Black in America has been removed from reading lists for allegedly being "anti-police" and promoting a particular political viewpoint.

Stamped: Racism, Antiracism, and You
by Jason Reynolds and Ibram X. Kendi (2020)
Challenged for: Political viewpoint, selective storytelling, author's public statements

This young adult adaptation of Kendi's "Stamped from the Beginning" traces the history of racist ideas in America. It has been challenged as part of broader attempts to ban discussions of systemic racism in schools.

New Kid
by Jerry Craft (2019)
Challenged for: Critical race theory, discussion of racism

This Newbery Medal-winning graphic novel about a Black student's experiences at a predominantly white private school has been challenged for addressing racism and microaggressions, despite being appropriate for its middle-grade audience.

Born a Crime: Stories from a South African Childhood
by Trevor Noah (2016)
Challenged for: Profanity, mature themes, racial discussions

Noah's memoir about growing up in apartheid and post-apartheid South Africa has been challenged for its frank discussions of race, colonialism, and poverty, as well as its language.

The 1619 Project: Born on the Water
by Nikole Hannah-Jones and Renée Watson (2021)
Challenged for: Critical race theory, historical inaccuracy, political viewpoint

This children's book explaining American slavery history has been caught up in broader political battles over how slavery and racism should be taught in schools.

American Born Chinese
by Gene Luen Yang (2006)
Challenged for: Racial stereotypes, offensive language, religious viewpoint

This groundbreaking graphic novel exploring the Asian American experience has been challenged for its use of stereotypical imagery, even though the book explicitly addresses and critiques these stereotypes.
